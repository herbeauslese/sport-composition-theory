# Sport Composition Theory

*A comprehensive, precise, and slightly pedantic framework for decomposing any sport into its fundamental components.*

---

## Introduction

It begins, as most inconvenient ideas do, at lunch. Someone — emboldened by a second coffee — proposed that chess and weightlifting are, at their core, the same kind of thing. The table fell silent. Then the arguing started.

The question is deceptively simple: what, precisely, *is* a sport made of? Not in the sociological sense, nor the philosophical one, but in the practical, compositional sense. If you were to break any given sport apart at the seams, what pieces would fall out? And — crucially — would the same pieces always appear?

We believe the answer is yes. Every sport, without exception, can be understood as a combination of four fundamental components. Their proportions vary wildly. But they are always there.

> *"Every sport is the same sport. The ratios are just different."*

We wrote up our initial conclusions on a napkin. We have since upgraded to this webpage.

---

## The Theory

Sport Composition Theory posits that any competitive sport can be fully described by four components. These components are **exhaustive** — nothing meaningful falls outside them — and **mutually exclusive** — no element of a sport belongs to more than one.

### Tactics

Decision-making, planning, reading opponents, and adapting strategy in real time. Tactics covers everything that happens above the neck: the choice of formation, the read of a situation, the decision to attack or to wait. It is the component most invisible to the casual observer, and most decisive to the serious one.

### Physical

The demands placed on the human body: strength, endurance, speed, agility, and their combinations. This component is perhaps the most intuitive — it is what most people picture when they picture an athlete. It is also, in many sports, less dominant than assumed.

### Skill

Learned human technique: motor control, precision, coordination, and execution. Skill is distinct from Physical in that it concerns the *quality* of movement, not the *capacity* for it. A sprinter's physical output is their top speed. Their skill is the technique that gets them there efficiently.

### Equipment

The role of external tools, machines, or materials in producing competitive outcomes.

Most sports involve some equipment — shoes, balls, rackets. For the purposes of this theory, Equipment only counts as a meaningful component when the *quality or specification* of the equipment has a measurable effect on competitive outcome. A tennis racket is not Equipment in this sense. A Formula 1 car is.

This distinction eliminates a great deal of pedantic edge cases while preserving the genuinely interesting ones. It is not perfect. We have made peace with this.

---

## Methodology

For each sport, a fixed set of guiding questions is applied — one per component. The answers are guided by available evidence where it exists, and by carefully reasoned argument where it does not.

**Tactics:** How much does a superior strategic approach — independent of physical ability — determine the outcome of a contest?

**Physical:** How much does a measurable difference in athletic conditioning affect the result, holding all else equal?

**Skill:** How much is the outcome determined by the precision and quality of learned human technique?

**Equipment:** How much would a significant disadvantage in equipment — independent of the athlete — change the result?

The resulting values are curated estimates. We do not pretend otherwise. The point is not false precision — it is structured comparison. A sport in which Tactics accounts for 50% and one in which it accounts for 5% are genuinely, meaningfully different things, even if the exact number is disputed.

*We are not scientists. We are, however, extremely opinionated. This is close enough.*
